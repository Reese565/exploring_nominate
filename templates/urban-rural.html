The Urban-Rurual Ideological Split

Geography and Culture

Today in many industrialized democracies it is common knowledge that rural areas lean conservative while cities tend to be more liberal. This is true for the United States as well, with Democrats tending to be concentrated in cities and Republicans to be more spread out across suburbs and rural areas. While the chasm of misunderstanding and animosity between rural and urban voters is one of the oldest divisions in American life, when it started to take a significant hold of our politics is a question worth exploring.

Methods and Data

To infer the point of division between urban and rural regions of the country, we need data regarding the population density of each congressional district going back to 1900. Unfortuantely, these data do not exist, but we can make a good guess. Each congressional district in the United States is suppose to represent an equal portion of the population at each US Census period. We can then divide the total US population for each year by the number of seats in the House of Representatives at the time to get a good estimate of how many people live in each district. Then, ignoring the fact that individuals can relacate between these census period, divide the population of each congressional district by the area each district covers, giving us an estimate of its population density. This can be calculated using GeoJSON files of each congressional district found here. We then combine our NOMINATE ideology data for each representative occupying a given congressional district starting in 1900 to match with our population density estimate.

With estimates of each congressional districts population desnity now matched with their ideology scores, we then group the data by year according to their corresponding quartile by population density. The first qunetile, Q1, is the bottom 25% of the congressional districts by population density. This would be the least populated congressional district in the US up to the 25th percentile, or the bttom 25%. We do the for the second quartile, Q2, which is the congressional district at the 25th percentile to the 50th percentile, or the median. We do this same procedure for the third quartile, Q3, grouping the 50th to the 75th percentiles, and Q4 representing the 75th to the 100th percentile and the most densly populated congressiional districts.

Here we can see that the most densley populated comgressional districts broke away from the other around the year 1935, during the Great Depression. Those less densely populated districts drift more ideological conservativism over the same period.
