What Divides Us

Congress Ideological Scores Over Time

With an effort to shed light into the divisiveness in Congress in recent years, in this section we take a top-down approach. First we begin by displaying average ideology scores per Congress in recent administrations. Afterward, we illustrate the House majority in the House of representatives throughout the duration of these administrations. Later we specify the scores of each congress member to illustrate divisiveness at the lowest level, as well as their stance per selected Bill topic. Lastly, we put House majority, overall ideology scores and Bill topic ideology scores per congress member, to illustrate the changes in ideology per selected administration.

In the visualization below it is evident that the Republican Party has been steadily holding their ideology scores closer to the conservative mark (1). In contrast, the Democratic Party has been steadily fluctuating around the ideology score -0.3 but always below -0.4. When comparing the party's overall scores, the Republican Party is more right-leaning and is less prone to fluctuate their ideology scores like the Democratic Party. This lack of fluctuation in ideology scores may reveal some political contention between parties. This observation brings to question what are the factors that contribute to this lack of fluctuation, and why do Democrats seem to be the party that is most likely to 'compromise' left-leaning views in each Congress held?

Below you can change the view of the visualization by selecting different Administrations and ideology scores. The drop down list gives you all the available administration options.

House Majority in Congress

A potential contributor to having congress members with scores closer to the extrenmes is House majority. The great amount of red in the tree map below demonstrates the party that had the majority of seats in the House of Representatives in the written time frame. One may assume that having House majority will shoot a party’s ideology score up to -1 or 1. (Refer to the average ideological scores in the previous visualization to follow). While this may be expected for some cases, it is surprising to see that the average ideology score when Democratic Party held House majority (116th congress), the overall ideology score still remained around the same as in previous years. This pattern brings to question whether having House majority influences the ideology of congress members or vice versa. From the previous visualization, we can see that Republicans tend to get more conservative when they don’t have House majority. Democrats remain the same despite the changes in majority.

In the tree map below you can select the Administration of your choice to see the House majority at that time. You can also highlight the House majority to illustrate the difference in leadership throughout the years, and how this factor may potentially influence political contention and unchanging ideology scores amongst congress members.

Congress member Ideology Score per Administration

Despite the overall ideology score trends highlighted in the previous visualizations, here we analyze the scores of congress members in recent administrations. It is evident that the scores of congress members may vary, but the main takeaway here is that the scores of each member are the main contributor for the overall ideological score average in a given time frame. Consider the ideology score of congressman Andrew Biggs (displayed), as opposed to democrats Neil Abercrombie and Frank Ballance. Congressman Biggs' score is much more right-leaning in comparison to Abercrombie's and Ballance. If the overall ideologies of each member follows this same pattern, it is evident how scores from Republicans are usually higher than Democrats.

Congress member Ideology per Topic

In the visualization below we selected topics that may heavily distinguish the overall ideology scores in congress. Each datapoint is the average ideological score of a congress member per selected topic and administration. Notice how topics such as “Abortion” create more political contention, given that the spikes in Republican members get closer to 1. As opposed to education, where throughout recent administrations the scores have remained somewhat constant.

Throughout this section, we have illustrated the factors that influence the divisiveness amongst house representatives: member stance is truly the major influencer, as each member’s ideology score is what carries weight in the House of Representatives when a specific party is at majority. For example, if each member from the Republican Party has ideology scores around 0.5, and they are already at House majority, their lack of political compromise heavily impacts the Bills passed and created. While we have shown evidence that there is divisiveness per topic in recent administrations, one must also consider the political climate at the chosen time frame.
